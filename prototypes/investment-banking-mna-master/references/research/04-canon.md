## Section 1 — Canonical Books

### B01 — Rosenbaum & Pearl, *Investment Banking: Valuation, LBOs, M&A, and IPOs* (3rd ed.)
**Status**: Tier-1 required reading | surrogate_primary: [T04-S001]

- The single most-used desk reference for breaking into and surviving on a deal desk. Sold 250k+ copies, used in 200+ universities; the de facto syllabus for analyst training. evidence: [T04-S001]
- Structured around the four core valuation methodologies that map 1:1 to the daily job: **comparable companies (trading comps)**, **precedent transactions**, **DCF**, and **LBO analysis**, plus full **M&A (buy-side/sell-side) and IPO** modeling. Ships 6 downloadable Excel templates. evidence: [T04-S001]
- Why canon: it is procedural, not theoretical — it teaches the *mechanics* (how to scrub a comp set, calendarize financials, build a football field) the way they're actually done on Wall Street. Pair with Damodaran for the *why* and the critique. evidence: [T04-S001]

### B02 — Koller, Goedhart, Wessels (McKinsey), *Valuation: Measuring and Managing the Value of Companies* (8th ed.)
**Status**: Tier-1 required reading | surrogate_primary: [T04-S002]

- McKinsey's #1-selling corporate-valuation guide, ~30 years in print. The authority on **value creation via ROIC and growth**, not just deal mechanics. evidence: [T04-S002]
- Core thesis (load-bearing): value is driven by **return on invested capital (ROIC) and growth**, and acquisitions create value only when the acquirer can raise the target's cash flows beyond what its own management/market already expects. 8th ed adds digital, ESG, long-term investing. evidence: [T04-S002]
- Complements Rosenbaum/Pearl: McKinsey = strategic/economic logic of value; R&P = banker execution.

### B03 — Brealey, Myers, Allen (+ Edmans), *Principles of Corporate Finance*
**Status**: Tier-2 foundation | reference: [T04-S028]

- The standard graduate corporate-finance textbook; supplies the theory underneath everything M&A: NPV, WACC, CAPM, capital structure, options. 14th ed. (2022). evidence: [T04-S028]
- Use as the conceptual backstop for *why* DCF/WACC work; not a deal manual. evidence: [T04-S028]

### B04 — Bruce Wasserstein, *Big Deal*
**Status**: Tier-2 history/lore | reference: [T04-S027]

- Insider M&A history by a legendary practitioner (head of M&A at First Boston; founder of Wasserstein Perella; worked 1,000+ deals). Encyclopedic war-stories and player biographies of 20th-century deals. evidence: [T04-S027]
- Value: dealmaking *culture, tactics, and ego* — the human layer comps/models omit. Better as reference than cover-to-cover. evidence: [T04-S027]

### B05 — Damodaran, *Narratives and Numbers* (companion to his blog/notes)
**Status**: Tier-1 mindset | verified_primary blog basis: [T04-S010]

- Reframes valuation as **a bridge between stories and numbers**: "every story becomes a number in the valuation and every number has a story behind it." Antidote to spreadsheet false precision. evidence: [T04-S010]

## Section 2 — The Valuation Canon: Aswath Damodaran (NYU Stern, free primary goldmine)

Damodaran ("the Dean of Valuation") posts his entire valuation/corp-finance course — lecture-note packets, datasets, spreadsheets, blog, podcasts — free at `pages.stern.nyu.edu/~adamodar` and `aswathdamodaran.blogspot.com`. This is the highest-value verified_primary corpus in the field.

### D01 — Three valuation approaches (the spine)
**verified_primary**: [T04-S003][T04-S012]

- Damodaran's method = **intrinsic (DCF)** + **relative (multiples)** + **option pricing** for embedded optionality. evidence: [T04-S003][T04-S012]
- **Intrinsic / DCF**: value = PV of expected cash flows, with risk in the cash flows or the discount rate. Packet 1 is the canonical free DCF reference. evidence: [T04-S003]
- **Relative valuation tells you what the market thinks; intrinsic tells you what the business is worth — they are not the same, and the gap is where opportunity lives.** evidence: [T04-S003][T04-S009]

### D02 — Price vs Value (the central distinction practitioners must internalize)
**verified_primary**: [T04-S009]

- **Price ≠ value.** The "pricing game" measures earnings/multiples against expectations; it's unpredictable (earnings are manipulable, unclear whose expectations matter, reactions vary). The "value game" requires an intrinsic narrative. evidence: [T04-S009]
- Maps directly to the deal-side three-layer construct below (price vs value vs deal value).

### D03 — Control premium (critical — and contested)
**verified_primary**: [T04-S005][T04-S004]

- Control has value only when there is something to fix: value of control = (value of company optimally run) − (value as currently run). If a firm is already well-run, the control premium should be small. evidence: [T04-S005][T04-S004]
- **CONTRADICTION TO PRESERVE**: practitioners routinely pay 20–40% control premiums regardless [T04-S020][T04-S021]; Damodaran argues much of that premium "has nothing to do with synergy, control or strategy" and "may just reflect the egos of acquiring CEOs." Do NOT soften this. evidence: [T04-S004]

### D04 — Synergy (the most over-claimed, least-delivered number)
**verified_primary**: [T04-S006][T04-S011]

- "Synergy is so seldom delivered in acquisitions because it is incorrectly valued, inadequately planned for, and much more difficult to create in practice than to compute on paper." evidence: [T04-S006][T04-S011]
- "Neither synergy nor control seems to show up in real deals as often (or as large an amount) as they do on paper." Treat any synergy line in a merger model as an aspiration to be discounted, not a fact. evidence: [T04-S006]

### D05 — "Just say no" / the seven sins of acquisitions
**verified_primary**: [T04-S007][T04-S008]

- Damodaran's acquisition-skeptic stance: acquirers overpay especially in **competitive/multi-bidder** auctions (winner's curse); large acquisitions are among the most reliable value-destroyers. He cites Kodak/Sterling Drug — overpaid ~$2.2B, "beginning of the end." evidence: [T04-S007][T04-S008]
- The "seven sins" lecture catalogs the recurring behavioral/process failures (overconfidence, debt-fueled empire building, paying with overvalued stock, etc.). evidence: [T04-S007]

## Section 3 — Deal Reality, Critique & Evidence (counterweight to the brochure)

### C01 — M&A destroys value more often than not (cite carefully)
**secondary**: [T04-S024]

- **NUMBER — cite source, not as universal law**: A KPMG study found **57.2% of acquirers ultimately destroyed shareholder value**; deals that looked promising (avg +13.2% TSR pre-close) saw TSR fall an avg 7.4% over the two years after close. ~42.8% did unlock meaningful synergies. evidence: [T04-S024]
- The widely-quoted "70–90% of M&A fails" is a **soft consensus / range across studies**, NOT a single rigorous figure — definitions of "fail" vary (TSR vs accretion vs synergy realization). Flag this whenever it's used. evidence: [T04-S024]
- Root causes (consistent across sources): overestimating benefits → **overpayment**, and failing to operationalize projected gains (integration). evidence: [T04-S024]

### C02 — Matt Levine / *Money Stuff* (how to read the deal underneath the press release)
**secondary/surrogate_primary**: [T04-S013][T04-S014]

- Daily Bloomberg Opinion column by an ex-Goldman equity-derivatives banker and ex-Wachtell M&A lawyer — uniquely credentialed to deconstruct *why* deals are structured the way they are (incentives, agency, legal mechanics). evidence: [T04-S013][T04-S014]
- Canon for the *interpretive* skill: seeing the conflict, the option, the regulatory arbitrage hidden in a transaction — not the modeling. evidence: [T04-S014]

### C03 — Howard Marks memos (price = risk; cycles)
**secondary (via reporting; primary memos at oaktreecapital.com)**

- "The most important single element in shaping investment risk is the **price** at which the investment is acquired. High prices imply high risk, low prices low risk." Directly relevant to deal discipline / not overpaying at cycle tops.
- Second-level thinking ("what's already priced in, how might others be wrong?") and margin of safety — the buy-side temperament that resists deal fever.

### C04 — Fairness opinions (structural conflict of interest — preserve the critique)
**secondary**: [T04-S022][T04-S023]

- A fairness opinion is a banker's written view that deal consideration is "fair, from a financial point of view." Rose to prominence after *Smith v. Van Gorkom* (1985). evidence: [T04-S022]
- **CONTRADICTION TO PRESERVE**: inherent conflict — the same bank advising the deal (and earning a success/financing fee contingent on closing) also opines it's "fair," giving a strong incentive to bless it. Delaware courts have repeatedly scrutinized this (Weinberger v. UOP: banker's large success fee undermined independence). Remedy trend: hire a separate independent advisor for the opinion. Do not present a fairness opinion as neutral truth. evidence: [T04-S022][T04-S023]

## Section 4 — Core Concepts (28)

Each is the practitioner vocabulary; primary mechanics in vendor tutorials, critique in Damodaran.

1. **DCF (discounted cash flow)** — value = PV of expected FCF. [T04-S003][T04-S001]
2. **FCF (free cash flow / FCFF vs FCFE)** — the cash the business throws off; FCFF→EV, FCFE→equity. [T04-S003]
3. **WACC (weighted average cost of capital)** — blended discount rate for FCFF. [T04-S003][T04-S028]
4. **Terminal value** — perpetuity-growth vs exit-multiple; usually 60–80% of DCF value → fragile. [T04-S001][T04-S003]
5. **Trading comps (comparable companies)** — relative valuation off a peer set. [T04-S001]
6. **EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/E** — the workhorse multiples. [T04-S001][T04-S020]
7. **Enterprise value vs equity value** — EV bridge (add debt, subtract cash). [T04-S001]
8. **Precedent transactions** — multiples paid in past deals; embed control premium. [T04-S001]
9. **Football field** — chart overlaying valuation ranges across methods. [T04-S001]
10. **LBO (leveraged buyout)** — buy with debt, repay from FCF, exit. [T04-S018]
11. **IRR (internal rate of return)** — PE's primary return metric. [T04-S018]
12. **MOIC / cash-on-cash multiple** — total return multiple, complements IRR. [T04-S018]
13. **Sources & Uses** — how the deal is funded vs what's paid for. [T04-S018]
14. **Accretion / dilution** — does pro-forma EPS rise (accretive) or fall (dilutive)? [T04-S015][T04-S016][T04-S017][T04-S019]
15. **Merger model** — combine financials, financing/purchase assumptions, output EPS impact. [T04-S015]
16. **Synergies (revenue vs cost)** — claimed deal value-add; chronically over-stated. [T04-S006][T04-S011]
17. **Control premium** — extra paid for control, typ. cited 20–40% (a *range*, not a law). [T04-S005][T04-S020][T04-S021]
18. **Purchase price allocation (PPA) / goodwill** — allocating price to assets; residual = goodwill. [T04-S001]
19. **Cash vs stock vs mixed consideration** — affects accretion, risk-sharing, control. [T04-S015][T04-S019]
20. **Price vs Value vs Deal Value (three layers)** — market price; intrinsic value; price actually paid incl. premium. [T04-S009][T04-S004]
21. **Winner's curse** — auction dynamics drive overpayment. [T04-S007][T04-S008]
22. **Fairness opinion** — banker's "financially fair" letter; conflicted. [T04-S022][T04-S023]
23. **Certainty / probability of close** — sellers/boards weigh deal certainty (financing, regulatory, MAC) against headline price. [T04-S029][T04-S030]
24. **CIM (Confidential Information Memorandum)** — banker-prepared sell-side marketing doc. [T04-S029]
25. **Teaser / NDA** — anonymized 1–2 pager → confidentiality agreement gate. [T04-S029]
26. **LOI / term sheet** — mostly non-binding deal terms (binding: exclusivity, confidentiality). [T04-S029]
27. **SPA / PSA (sale & purchase agreement)** — the binding definitive document; reps, warranties, MAC, conditions. [T04-S029]
28. **Success fee (Lehman / Double Lehman) + retainer** — advisory comp; typ. success fee ~2–8% scaled by deal size, plus offsetable retainer. [T04-S025][T04-S026]

**Numbers ledger (always cite, never universalize):**
- Control premium typically **20–40%** over unaffected price — a benchmark *range* across studies, not a constant. [T04-S020][T04-S021][T04-S031]
- ~**57.2%** of acquirers destroyed shareholder value (one KPMG study); "70–90% fail" = soft multi-study consensus, definition-dependent. [T04-S024]
- M&A advisory success fee ~**2–8%** of deal value (Lehman/Double-Lehman scaled), small/mid-market norms. [T04-S025][T04-S026]
- Analyst hours **~80–100/week** = widely cited industry lore, not a single sourced statistic — flag as anecdotal norm.
